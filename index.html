What is a thriving adolescent?

Meet the 5C’s of PYD: Qualities That Help Young People Thrive

Research has revealed that adolescents are at their best when they embody a set of five key qualities. These qualities are known as the Five C's of PYD and include building competence, caring for others, being confident in their own self and abilities, connecting with others, and having integrity with a strong character. While each of these qualities is valuable on its own, when an adolescent is able to cultivate all of them, they are more likely to meaningfully contribute to their communities in ways that support themselves and others.

Competence

Building skills and capabilities.

+

Competence


What is Competence?

Competence is all about being good at something. We’ve all felt the joy of learning and mastering a skill that really matters to us. Whether it’s a young person excelling in school, sports, or art, they build skills that help them not only in those areas but also in all the different situations they’ll face in life. Developing competence gives young people the confidence to handle their social, emotional, cognitive, and moral environments effectively and successfully. When young people feel confident in these areas, they can tackle everyday tasks like cooking with family, resolving conflicts with friends, or solving tricky math problems with ease.


Why does it matter?

A strong sense of competence allows youth to participate in their broader social world, ultimately contributing to their communities in ways that improve the well-being for both themselves and those around them. A young person who notices a peer in need and knows how to comfort them is a true social superhero! They make both the young person and their peers feel better and create a more positive world. Or, take a student who creates a study schedule and invites others to join. This kind of academic skill builds a solid foundation for a successful career when working with and leading others.


How can you build competence in your adolescent?

Competence is about helping your child develop and use their skills in real-life settings. A young person's competence grows when young people feel safe to experiment and try new things with encouragement and support from their parents and others. You can help your teen develop competence by showing them how to tell their story with agency in their successes and acknowledge redemption in their failures.

Caring

Developing empathy and compassion.

+

Caring


What is it?

Caring is all about having empathy and compassion for others. At its core, caring involves a sense of responsibility that is driven by prosocial behaviors. A caring adolescent feels empathy and sympathy for others and themselves while demonstrating a desire to help those in need. In other words, caring is linked to kindness.


Why does it matter?

When young people have a strong sense of care, they cultivate respect for others, which can have a significant positive impact on their lives and the lives of those around them. A teen who demonstrates caring can comfort a friend, show kindness toward their sibling or spend time volunteering. When adolescents engage in these kinds of activities, their communities become spaces where everyone has a chance to thrive.


How can you build caring in your adolescent?

Showing your teen respect, honesty, and trust can help them develop a caring heart. When you believe in their ability to be compassionate and caring, you inspire them to do the same and make a real difference in the world. Schools can also serve as a platform where such role models can be discovered when teachers and staff actively encourage young individuals to adopt diverse perspectives and collaborate with others.

Confidence

Building self-esteem and belief in oneself.

+

Confidence


What is it?

Confidence is like a superpower that lets young people believe in themselves and take on anything they set their minds to. It’s all about knowing that you have the potential to do amazing things, even if you haven’t tried them yet. And guess what? Confidence also makes you feel super confident and happy with yourself, so you can love and accept yourself just the way you are.


Why does it matter?

A strong sense of confidence allows youth to experience higher levels of hope and cultivate their purpose in life. When teens feel confident in themselves, they’re more likely to step outside their comfort zones and try new things. They trust that they have the skills and abilities to succeed. Raising their hand in class, meeting new people, and even auditioning for a play are all possible because of this confidence. They believe that they matter and have something valuable to offer.


How can you build confidence in your adolescent?

Confidence is all about helping your child develop a belief in themselves and their abilities. School can be a space where adolescents can explore leadership roles and connect with mentors that help them foster confidence. At home, encouraging your child to preserver even when things are difficult and allowing them the time and space to speak for themselves are great ways to build confidence. It's also especially important to foster confidence in young girls as they often find it more difficult to be confident in themselves than boys do.

Connection

Forming positive relationships with peers and adults.

+

Connection


What is it?

Connected teens feel safe, have a sense of belonging, and relate in positive ways to the people around them and in their communities. At it's core, connection is all about building healthy relationships with people who matter to us. These connections can be with family, friends, teachers, and other important people in our lives. What makes these relationships meaningful is that both people are contributing and feel like they are an important part of that relationship.


Why does it matter?

A sense of connection allows youth to perform better academically and contribute to their immediate and broader social worlds. Additionally, when youth feel a strong sense of connection, they are less likely to experience anxiety and more likely to open up about how they're doing in meaningful ways.


How can you build connection in your adolescent?

Connection happens over time. As young people develop skills like active listening, effective communication, and an understanding of mutual respect, they allow for opportunities of meaningful and healthy connections. Encouraging your child to join clubs, sports, and other activities they enjoy is a fantastic way for them to meet new people and build a strong sense of connection. Make sure to pay special attention to fostering connection in boys as they often find it more difficult to connect in these ways than girls do.

Character

Embracing integrity, responsibility, and moral values.

+

Character


What is it?

Teens with a strong sense of character respect others and have a well-developed understanding of the difference between right and wrong. Young people who demonstrate character are honest, guided by values, and take responsibility for their actions. In other words, when youth have a sense of character, they act with integrity and follow their moral compass.


Why does it matter?

Character helps young people navigate their worlds in ways that honor both themselves and others. A teen who stands up for a friend when they are bullied or returns a lost wallet shows a strong sense of character that ultimately makes the places and communities they are a part of a better space for everyone.


How can you build character in your adolescent?

Character can be developed in several ways. First, involving young people in decision-making and problem-solving gives them a chance to work through situations where character is needed. Second, when we provide youth with opportunities for civic engagement they develop a strong sense of social responsibility and allow them to contribute to their communities. Finally, adults who treat young people with respect and honor set good standards and become role models of character for them to follow. So, one of the easiest ways to help your child develop character is by demonstrating character yourself.

Thriving Adolescents Create Positive Change in Their Communities

When young people embody and exhibit the 5C's, they develop a natural desire to give back to others in their community. Researchers call this contribution. As young people demonstrate competence, confidence, connection, caring, and character they feel equipped and ready to contribute to the places and people that are helping to build these very qualities in them. Not only are youth equipped to contribute but they actively want to contribute in meaningful ways to make their world a better place.

This kind of desire involves giving back to themselves, their family and friends, their community, and even the broader society. Supporting and mentoring others, volunteering, joining youth-led organizations, leading a group of their own, and using their skills to improve community life are all ways that young people can demonstrate contribution. And what's more, contributing in these ways is seen as a hallmark of a healthy and thriving adolescent. In short, when youth feel that they have the abilities to make a difference, believe that their actions matter, develop a strong social network, are guided by ethical principles, and driven to serve out of empathy, they begin to feel a sense of purpose that leads them to benefit those around them.

How can you build these qualities in your adolescent?

Scientists have discovered a list of 40 crucial nutrients or building blocks that help young people develop the 5Cs. When young people have these building blocks (often called assets) as a solid foundation, they develop the qualities that make for a fulfilling life.

These assets are divided into two main categories that describe where each characteristic comes from. The first highlights the positive qualities a young person develops in themselves (internal assets), while the second recognizes the positive characteristics present in their community (external assets).

Within each category are four smaller groups that help youth grow into healthy adults. As internal assets, they need a love for learning, positive values, strong social skills, and a sense of self‑worth. While powerful on their own, they become even more life‑giving when coupled with external assets like support, empowerment, clear boundaries and expectations, and ways for youth to constructively use their time. Each of these smaller categories holds a number of assets that contribute to youth developing in those areas.

Several youth programs have been developed to help adolescents build internal strengths within supportive environments. These include mentorship initiatives, after‑school programs, and leadership camps that often focus on a targeted set of assets. While valuable, such programs rely heavily on the time, transportation, and consistency parents and caregivers provide. Yet there are moments outside of formal programs—after practice, during weekends, in everyday conversations—when meaningful growth still happens. With so many potential assets to nurture, it’s natural to wonder: which ones should we focus on first?

Curiosity: The Hidden Strength Behind Youth Thriving

Of all the assets a young person can develop, curiosity might be one of the most powerful motivators for positive youth development—and the most overlooked. In youth programs, curiosity is usually implicit, for instance, as part of cognitive competence or a child’s commitment to learning. While PYD frameworks emphasize intrinsic growth and exploration, they haven’t traditionally highlighted curiosity as a distinct strength. However, curiosity is the spark that makes a teen ask questions, explore new interests, and connect what they’re learning to the world around them. Studies show that curious adolescents are more engaged at school, more resilient during challenges, and more open to building meaningful relationships. Curiosity is something every person is born with. Even more encouraging is that it’s a strength that grows when adults make space for wonder in everyday life—at the dinner table, in the car ride home, or during a walk through the neighborhood.

What is Curiosity?

We all know what it feels like to be curious about something or someone. But what truly defines curiosity? Often, it’s associated with a desire to know or seek something new. Researchers have identified two key components that contribute to the experience of curiosity. The first is called stretching, the active pursuit of the new or unfamiliar that motivates us to bridge knowledge gaps. The second is embracing, a willingness to accept that life is full of uncertainty. Together, these two elements create a positive motivator for young people, encouraging them to explore their world by seeking new opportunities for growth while acknowledging and accepting the uncertainties inherent in everyday life. As a result, curious adolescents are less likely to experience high levels of anxiety and approach challenges with openness.

Types of Curiosity

Researchers have also identified two primary types of curiosity. The first type is interest‑type curiosity, where the pursuit of information is positively motivating and exploration is enjoyable for its own sake. The second is deprivation‑type curiosity, driven by an urgent need to resolve information gaps that cause discomfort or anxiety so a person can function effectively.

Can Risk Taking Be Positive?

Adolescence is a time brimming with curiosity, marked by exploration and a natural inclination toward novelty‑seeking. This period is also characterized by risk‑taking, often perceived as negative behaviors. However, research reveals a spectrum: positive risk‑taking includes socially acceptable, constructive risks—trying out for a sports team, enrolling in a challenging course, or leading a new club. While outcomes are uncertain, potential costs are low, and benefits can be significant. Curiosity fuels these positive risks, encouraging teens to stretch their abilities and grow. Although curiosity hasn’t traditionally been named among the 5Cs or the 40 Developmental Assets, it quietly—and crucially—supports both frameworks.

Curiosity can drive teenagers to explore positive risks that lead to activities that help them develop their inner strengths while also interacting with the outside world.

Ultimately, these experiences can lead to spending time in environments that foster the development of competence, confidence, caring, connection, and character. In other words, curiosity can be a powerful motivator for healthy development that helps your adolescent become the best version of themselves.
